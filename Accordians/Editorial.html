Frequently Asked Questions

Everything you need to know, laid out with clarity and care.

  • Editorial design endures because it prioritizes clarity, hierarchy, and rhythm above all else. The whitespace breathes, the typography speaks before you read a word, and every element earns its place on the page. Great editorial work doesn't follow trends — it sets them.
    Design
  • Serifs create a visual flow between letters, guiding the eye across long passages of text. In print traditions stretching back centuries, serifs have proven to reduce fatigue in extended reading. Paired with generous leading and measured line length, they create an almost meditative reading experience.
    Typography
  • Negative space is not emptiness — it is intentional silence. It gives the eye a place to rest, creates emphasis through contrast, and communicates confidence. Designs cluttered with content signal anxiety; designs that breathe signal authority and trust.
    Layout
  • Absolutely. The most expressive work often uses the fewest elements. A single well-chosen accent color, one dominant typeface, one recurring rule — these create a design language stronger than a dozen competing elements. Constraints breed creativity.
    Philosophy
  • Pure white (#ffffff) creates harsh contrast that fatigues the eye under extended reading. Warm paper tones like cream, ecru, or parchment reduce glare and evoke trust — they signal heritage, editorial authority, and the timeless permanence of print. The physical world informs the digital.
